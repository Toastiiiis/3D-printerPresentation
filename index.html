3D printing


What is 3D printing

  • Procress of additive manufacturing
    • Adding material layer by layer to create a 3D object
  • Allows for creation of a nearly unlimited amount of 3D structures
  • Used in personal and commercial settings

Why should you care about it?

From model to object

Modelling

A 3D object is modelled using software.
Objects consist of hundreds of interlinked faces, vertices and edges.


Software commonly used:
Fusion 360, Auto CAD
Tinkercad, Blender

[1] 3D Modelling in Blender

Slicing

The modelled 3D object is turned into g-code, which 3D printers can interpret.
G-code is a text document full of coordinates, understood by the printer.


Software commonly used:
Ultimaker Cura, PrusaSlicer
Simplify3D, OctoPrint

[2] Slicing in Ultimaker Cura

Printing

Following the g-code, the printer will move on and x,y and z axis, layering filament in the paths specified by the g-code.

Popular beginner printers:
Prusa Mini+, Creality Ender 3
Anycubic Mega Zero

[3] Printing on a Creality Ender 3

{1} Baby Groot - 3D Printing Time Lapse

Sounds simple, right?

Before printing can begin, several steps need to be taken

    Levelling the printing bed

    The print bed needs to be readjusted using the adjustment screws to ensure proper adhesion of the print.

    [4] 3D printer anatomy

    Heating the nozzle

    The nozzle must be heated to the temperatures at which it can melt the filament and press it through the nozzle, creating a layer.

    Common nozzle temperatures used:
    ABS: 230-260 °C
    PETG: 210-250 °C
    Nylon: 180-260 °C
    PLA: 190-220°C

    [5] 3D printer nozzle

    Heating the bed

    The print bed must be heated to 60-85°C in order to allow the bottom layer of the Filament to stay warm and adhere to the bed.

    [6] 3D printer bed

    Common procedures

    FDM - Fused Deposition Modeling

    A procress which extrudes molten plastic from a nozzle, layering it until the part is completed.
    Used by beginners and professionals alike

    [7] FDM printer

    SLA - Stereolithography

    A process which uses artifical resin that is hardened in layers by shooting a light/laser one coordinate at a time at the screen seperating resin and laser.

    [8] SLA printer

    DLP - Digital Light Processing

    A process similar to SLA, with the key difference being that each layer is hardened as a whole using a digital screen instead of a laser shooting individual coordinates

    [9] DLP printer

    Kinds of Filaments

    PLA

    PLA is the filament of choice for most FDM printers, as it features high strength and stiffness as well as a low melting point.

    ABS

    ABS is used for its high temperature resistance (-20 - 80°C) and great strength.

    Nylon

    Nylon is used due to it's high flexbility and durability.

    PETG

    PETG sees use in robotics as it combines strength and wear resistance.

    The future of 3D printing

    Metal printing

    Printing with metal is currently undergoing tests and slowly being adopted into industry.

    It offers advantages such as:
    • Extremely high strength
    • Minimal material waste in comparism to subtractive processes
    • Extended geometrical capabilities

    {2} 3D Printing an INSANE Steel Part that's IMPOSSIBLE to Make on a CNC Machine

    But how did this just work?

    How did this just work?

    0. Preparation

    Metal powder is filtered, inerted, sealed and finally transported to the printer.

    [10] Metal Powder Preparation

    1. Deposition

    Metal Powder is moved into the inert (non-reactive) gas filled printing chamber and spread onto the print plate.

    [11] Metal Printer Deposition

    2. Hardening

    One or multiple lasers are shot at the deposited metal powder to melt it.

    [12] Metal Printer Hardening

    3. Repeat

    Repeat the process layer by layer until the part is done.

    [13] Metal Printing Process

    4. Powder Reclamation

    The Powder is sucked back into a tank which undergoes the same procedure as in step 0, the cycle repeats.

    [14] Metal Powder Reclamation

    Concrete printing

    3D printing with concrete is currently being experimented with and has been used for "printing" houses

    Sadly it is nowhere close to being viable due to:
    • Manual finishing being required
    • Long setup time
    • High equipment cost

    {3} 3D Printed Concrete House, Printed in 48 hours

    Notice a pattern?

    Most processes are similar

    Here, concrete is extruded from the nozzle, but essentially it is just an FDM process without plastic

    [15] Concrete printing

    Conclusion

    Overall, 3D printing offers many useful features at costs as low as 150€:

    • A hobby with a gradual learning curve
    • Cheap, unattended prototyping
    • Limits only set by your own imagination

    Additionally, it is bound expand it's use from hobbyists to:

    • Medicine
    • Food industry
    • Space travel
